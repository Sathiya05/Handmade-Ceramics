Material Ethics

Earth as it
is intended.

We don't buy pre-mixed clay from a factory. By sourcing raw minerals and mixing our own clay bodies and glazes, we maintain total control over the footprint and performance of every piece.

Traceable Minerals

Every oxide, feldspar, and silica grain is sourced from suppliers with clear extraction practices. We know exactly where our materials come from.

Lead-Free Promise

The dinnerware we create is designed to hold your food for a lifetime. All our glazes are third-party tested to be 100% food-safe and lead-free.

The Studio Methodology

The Six Stages of Creation

Stage 01 · Day 1

Approx. piece temperature

Room temp

Soft, wet clay — entirely reworkable.

01

Wedging — preparing the clay

Before anything is shaped, the clay has to be made trustworthy. We knead and slam each block against the wedging table in a spiral motion, forcing out air pockets that would otherwise turn into steam explosions in the kiln.

  • Local stoneware clay, sourced from a single supplier for consistency
  • Roughly 15 minutes of wedging per kilogram of clay
  • Reclaimed trimmings are re-wedged back into this stage
02

Throwing — on the wheel

Each piece is centred, opened, and pulled up individually. A maker throwing dinner plates will check rim diameter and wall thickness against the batch master every few pieces — close enough to feel like a set, never identical enough to feel like a mould.

  • Wheel speed: 120–180 RPM depending on the form
  • A skilled maker throws 15–20 mugs or 8–10 plates per session
  • Pieces dry slowly under plastic to reach "leather-hard"
03

Trimming & stamping

Once leather-hard, pieces go back on the wheel upside down to be trimmed — refining the foot, shaving excess weight, and creating the clean ring base every KlinCraft piece stands on. This is also when each piece receives the studio stamp and a scratched batch number.

  • Mug handles are pulled by hand and attached at this stage
  • The maker's initials and batch number are stamped into the foot
  • Trimmings are collected and returned to the wedging table
04

Bisque firing

Fully dried pieces go into the kiln for their first firing, reaching around 950°C over roughly eight hours. This drives out the last of the chemical water in the clay and hardens it into "bisque" — strong enough to handle, but still porous enough to drink in glaze.

  • Slow ramp-up prevents cracking from trapped moisture
  • Bisque pieces are inspected and lightly sanded smooth
  • Any hairline cracks are caught here, before glaze is wasted

factory-cast

Typical factory-cast

Slip-cast in moulds — every unit identical
Glazes mixed in-house from raw minerals
Commercial pre-mixed glazes, sprayed by machine
Fired in small batches of ~140 pieces, gas kiln

Both can be food-safe and durable — the difference is in the hands, the time, and the small imperfections that make a piece feel like yours.
